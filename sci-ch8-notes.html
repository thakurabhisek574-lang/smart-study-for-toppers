PAGE 1
Introduction to Heredity
Heredity: The transmission of characters (traits) from parents to offspring.
Variation: The differences in the characters among the individuals of a species. Even "identical" twins have small variations.
Accumulation: Variations during reproduction are important. In asexual reproduction, variations are very few. In sexual reproduction, variations are large and get accumulated over generations.
PAGE 2
Mendel & His Experiments
Gregor Johann Mendel: Known as the "Father of Genetics". He used Garden Pea (Pisum sativum) for his experiments.
Why Pea Plant?
1. Short life cycle.
2. Clear contrasting traits (Tall/Dwarf, Round/Wrinkled).
3. Easy to grow and cross-pollinate.
PAGE 3
Monohybrid Cross (One Trait)
Mendel crossed a Pure Tall (TT) and a Pure Dwarf (tt) plant.
F1 Generation: All plants were Tall (Tt). No dwarf plants.
F2 Generation: (On self-pollinating F1) Plants were produced in 3:1 ratio (3 Tall, 1 Dwarf).
Ratios:
• Phenotypic Ratio = 3:1
• Genotypic Ratio = 1:2:1 (TT : Tt : tt)
PAGE 4
Dihybrid Cross (Two Traits)
Crossing plants with two traits: Round Yellow seeds (RRYY) and Wrinkled Green seeds (rryy).
F1 Generation: All were Round Yellow (RrYy).
F2 Generation Ratio: 9:3:3:1. This proved that traits are inherited independently.
PAGE 5
Dominant & Recessive Traits
Dominant Trait: The trait that expresses itself in the F1 generation (e.g., Tallness 'T').
Recessive Trait: The trait that remains hidden in F1 but appears in F2 (e.g., Dwarfness 't').
PAGE 6
How Traits Get Expressed?
DNA provides information for making Proteins. A section of DNA that provides info for one protein is called a Gene.
If the protein (enzyme) works efficiently, the trait is expressed fully (e.g., more growth hormone = Tall plant).
PAGE 7
Mechanism of Inheritance
Each cell has two copies of each chromosome (one from each parent).
Germ cells (gametes) take only one chromosome from each pair. When they combine, the full set of chromosomes is restored in the zygote.
PAGE 8
Sex Determination in Humans
Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes. 22 pairs are Autosomes. 1 pair is the Sex Chromosome.
Females: Have a perfect pair of XX.
Males: Have a mismatched pair of XY (Y is smaller).
PAGE 9
Probability of Child's Sex
All children inherit an 'X' chromosome from the mother.
The sex of the child depends on what they inherit from the father.
• If Father gives X → Girl (XX).
• If Father gives Y → Boy (XY).
Note: In humans, the father is responsible for the sex of the child, not the mother.
PAGE 10
Acquired vs Inherited Traits
Inherited Traits: Passed from parents to offspring via DNA (e.g., Eye color).
Acquired Traits: Developed during life, not passed to next generation (e.g., learning a language, weight loss due to starvation). These do not change the DNA of germ cells.

-- LONG QUESTIONS --

LONG Q1
How do Mendel's experiments show that traits may be dominant or recessive?

Answer: Mendel crossed pure tall (TT) pea plants with pure dwarf (tt) pea plants.
1. In the F1 generation, all plants were tall. This showed that only the 'Tall' trait expressed itself, making it the Dominant trait.
2. The 'Dwarf' trait did not disappear but was hidden.
3. In the F2 generation (by selfing F1), dwarf plants reappeared in a 3:1 ratio. This proved that the 'Dwarf' trait was Recessive and only expresses when both alleles are 'tt'.

LONG Q2
How do Mendel's experiments show that traits are inherited independently?

Answer: This was shown by the Dihybrid Cross (using two traits: seed shape and color).
1. Mendel crossed Round-Yellow (RRYY) with Wrinkled-Green (rryy) plants.
2. In F1, all were Round-Yellow (RrYy).
3. In F2, he got four types: Round-Yellow, Round-Green, Wrinkled-Yellow, and Wrinkled-Green in the ratio 9:3:3:1.
4. The appearance of new combinations (Round-Green and Wrinkled-Yellow) proved that seed shape and seed color are inherited independently of each other.

LONG Q3
Explain the mechanism of sex determination in human beings.

Answer: In humans, sex is determined genetically:
1. Females have two identical sex chromosomes: XX.
2. Males have two different sex chromosomes: XY.
3. All eggs produced by a mother carry an X chromosome.
4. Sperms produced by a father are of two types: 50% carry X and 50% carry Y.
5. If a sperm with X fertilizes the egg → Child is Female (XX).
6. If a sperm with Y fertilizes the egg → Child is Male (XY).
Thus, the father's sperm determines the sex of the baby.

LONG Q4
What are the different ways in which individuals with a particular trait may increase in a population?

Answer: Traits can increase via:
1. Natural Selection: If a trait provides a survival advantage (e.g., green color in beetles hiding from birds), it is naturally selected and increases.
2. Genetic Drift: An accidental change in gene frequency due to a random event (e.g., an elephant stepping on a bush) can increase a trait even if it has no survival advantage.
3. Variations: Small changes during DNA copying that might prove beneficial over time.

LONG Q5
Differentiate between Inherited and Acquired traits with examples.

Answer:
1. Inherited Traits: These are passed from parents to offspring through DNA in germ cells. They can be seen in future generations.
Ex: Eye color, Height, Attached earlobes.
2. Acquired Traits: These are developed by an individual during their lifetime due to environment or experiences. They do not change DNA of germ cells and are NOT passed to next generation.
Ex: Learning to dance, Scar on skin, Weight loss due to starvation.

LONG Q6
Explain Mendel’s Law of Segregation with an example.

Answer: This law states that every individual possesses two alleles for any particular trait, and these alleles separate (segregate) during gamete formation.
1. Example: In a tall plant with genotype Tt, the alleles 'T' and 't' do not mix.
2. When this plant produces gametes, 50% gametes carry 'T' and 50% carry 't'.
3. This ensures that each gamete receives only one allele. This is why it is also called the Law of Purity of Gametes.

LONG Q7
"Genes and chromosomes have a similar behavior." Justify this statement.

Answer: Genes are segments of DNA located on chromosomes. Their behavior is similar because:
1. Both occur in pairs in normal body cells (diploid).
2. Both segregate during gamete formation so that a gamete gets only one of the pair.
3. The independent assortment of chromosomes during meiosis leads to the independent assortment of genes. Thus, chromosomes are the actual 'vehicles' of heredity.

LONG Q8
Explain how the cellular DNA controls the phenotype (appearance) of an organism.

Answer:
1. DNA is the information source for making Proteins.
2. A specific Gene provides instructions for a specific protein (usually an enzyme).
3. Example: Plant height depends on a growth hormone. If the gene for that hormone is efficient, more hormone is produced, and the plant becomes Tall.
4. If the gene is altered (mutation) or inefficient, less hormone is produced, resulting in a Short plant. Thus, DNA controls traits via proteins.

LONG Q9
Why did Mendel choose the pea plant for his experiments? (Give 5 reasons)

Answer: Mendel chose Pisum sativum because:
1. It has distinct contrasting characters (Tall/Short, Round/Wrinkled).
2. It has a short life span, allowing him to study many generations in a short time.
3. It produces a large number of seeds in a single generation.
4. It is naturally self-pollinating but can be easily cross-pollinated.
5. It is easy to grow and maintain in a garden.

LONG Q10
What is a Punnett Square? Use it to show the F2 generation of a cross between Tt and Tt.

Answer: A Punnett Square is a graphical representation used to calculate the probability of all possible genotypes of offspring.
Cross: Tt x Tt
- Gametes: T and t | T and t
Punnett Square:
[ T ] [ t ]
[ T ] | TT | Tt |
[ t ] | Tt | tt |

Results:
- Genotypes: 1 TT, 2 Tt, 1 tt (1:2:1)
- Phenotypes: 3 Tall, 1 Dwarf (3:1)

-- HARDEST QUESTIONS --

HARD Q11
Explain how Mendelian factors (genes) are physically expressed in an organism. What is the role of enzymes in this process?

Answer: Genes express themselves by controlling the production of proteins.
1. DNA as Info Source: Cellular DNA is the information source for making proteins.
2. Gene to Enzyme: A section of DNA (gene) provides information for a specific enzyme (protein).
3. Enzyme Function: Enzymes catalyze biochemical reactions. For example, an enzyme triggers the production of growth hormones.
4. Trait Expression: If the enzyme works efficiently, more hormone is made, resulting in a tall plant. If the gene is altered, the enzyme becomes less efficient, less hormone is made, and the plant remains short.

HARD Q12
Work out a cross to show the inheritance of two pairs of contrasting traits (Dihybrid Cross). Mention the Phenotypic ratio of F2 generation.

Answer:
Parents: Round Yellow (RRYY) x Wrinkled Green (rryy)
Gametes: RY and ry
F1 Generation: RrYy (All Round Yellow)
F1 Selfing: RrYy x RrYy
F2 Generation Gametes: RY, Ry, rY, ry (from both parents).
Phenotypic Ratio: 9:3:3:1
- 9 Round Yellow
- 3 Round Green
- 3 Wrinkled Yellow
- 1 Wrinkled Green
This confirms the Law of Independent Assortment.

HARD Q13
Why is the ratio of boys and girls in a population usually 1:1? Explain with a genetic diagram.

Answer:
1. All human females produce ova (eggs) with only X chromosomes.
2. Males produce two types of sperms in equal proportions (50:50): one half carrying X and the other half carrying Y.
3. Random Fertilization: There is a 50% chance that an X-sperm will fertilize the egg (resulting in a girl, XX) and a 50% chance that a Y-sperm will fertilize it (resulting in a boy, XY).
4. Since the probability of X-sperm and Y-sperm meeting an egg is equal, the ratio in the population stays approximately 1:1.

HARD Q14
Only variations that confer an advantage to an individual organism will survive in a population. Do you agree? Why or why not?

Answer: Not necessarily.
1. Natural Selection: If a variation provides a survival advantage (like better camouflage), it will definitely be passed on and increase in the population.
2. Genetic Drift: However, some variations do not provide any advantage but still survive due to accidental changes in small populations. For example, a random event might kill off all members of a species except those with a specific neutral variation.
3. Conclusion: While advantageous variations are more likely to survive, neutral variations can also persist due to chance events or genetic drift.

HARD Q15
What is the 'Law of Independent Assortment'? Explain why this law does not hold true for genes located closely on the same chromosome.

Answer:
1. Definition: It states that when two pairs of traits are combined in a hybrid, the segregation of one pair of characters is independent of the other pair.
2. The Exception (Linkage): This law works because genes for different traits are usually on different chromosomes.
3. Why it fails: If two genes are located very close to each other on the same chromosome, they tend to be inherited together. This phenomenon is called Linkage. In such cases, the traits do not assort independently, and the 9:3:3:1 ratio is not obtained.

HARD Q16
Contrast 'Genotype' and 'Phenotype'. Can two organisms have the same phenotype but different genotypes? Illustrate with Mendel’s F2 generation.

Answer:
1. Genotype: The genetic constitution of an organism (e.g., TT, Tt).
2. Phenotype: The observable physical characteristics (e.g., Tall, Dwarf).
3. Same Phenotype, Different Genotype: Yes, it is possible due to Dominance.
4. Illustration: In Mendel's F2 generation, both TT (Homozygous Tall) and Tt (Heterozygous Tall) look exactly the same (Phenotype = Tall). However, their genetic makeup (Genotype) is different. This is why the phenotypic ratio is 3:1 while the genotypic ratio is 1:2:1.

HARD Q17
Explain the importance of 'Variation' in a species. How does sexual reproduction facilitate this better than asexual reproduction?

Answer:
1. Importance: Variations allow species to adapt to environmental changes (like global warming or new diseases). It prevents the entire species from being wiped out.
2. Sexual vs Asexual: In asexual reproduction, DNA copying is the only source of variation, which is very slow and minimal.
3. Sexual Advantage: It involves the fusion of gametes from two different individuals. This creates a new combination of DNA. Additionally, during meiosis (gamete formation), "crossing over" of chromosomes occurs, which reshuffles the genetic material, leading to high diversity.

HARD Q18
Why are traits acquired during the lifetime of an individual not inherited? Explain with the example of the tail-less mice experiment.

Answer:
1. The Principle: Only changes in the DNA of germ cells (gametes) are passed to the next generation. Changes in somatic (body) cells do not affect the DNA of the next generation.
2. Experiment: Scientist August Weismann cut off the tails of mice for 21 generations.
3. Observation: Even after 21 generations, the offspring were born with full tails.
4. Conclusion: Cutting the tail is an acquired change in somatic cells. It does not change the genes in the sperm or egg. Therefore, acquired traits are not inherited.
