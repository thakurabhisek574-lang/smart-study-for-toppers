CHAPTER 02: ACIDS, BASES AND SALTS
EXTREME DETAILED THEORY NOTES

1. Detailed Nature of Acids

Acids are chemical substances that taste sour and change blue litmus to red. They release H+ ions in aqueous solutions.

Hydronium Ion Concept:

Since $H^+$ ions cannot exist independently, they combine with water to form Hydronium ions ($H_3O^+$).

HCl(aq) + H₂O(l) → H₃O⁺(aq) + Cl⁻(aq)

Key Classifications:

  • Strong Acids: Completely ionize in water (e.g., $HCl, H_2SO_4$).
  • Weak Acids: Partially ionize in water (e.g., $CH_3COOH$, Citric acid).
  • Organic Acids: Naturally occurring (Lactic acid in curd).
2. Detailed Nature of Bases

Bases are bitter and feel soapy. They turn red litmus blue and release OH- ions.

NaOH(s) + H₂O(l) → Na⁺(aq) + OH⁻(aq)

Alkalis: Bases that are soluble in water are specifically called Alkalis.

4. Important Chemical Reactions

I. Acid + Metal:

Zn + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂ ↑

Hydrogen Test: Burns with a characteristic POP sound.

II. Neutralization:

Acid + Base → Salt + Water
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
⚠️ SAFETY FIRST: Always add Acid to Water. Never the other way around!
5. pH Scale in Everyday Life

The pH scale ranges from 0-14. 7 is Neutral.

1. Stomach: Uses HCl for digestion. Antacids cure acidity.
2. Tooth Decay: Starts if mouth pH < 5.5.
3. Bee Sting: Inject methanoic acid (use Baking Soda to neutralize).
6. Commercial Salts Formulas

1. Bleaching Powder: $CaOCl_2$ (Disinfectant).

2. Baking Soda: $NaHCO_3$ (Cooking & Antacid).

3. Washing Soda: $Na_2CO_3 \cdot 10H_2O$ (Hardness removal).

4. Plaster of Paris: $CaSO_4 \cdot \frac{1}{2}H_2O$ (Fractured bones).

SECTION D: CHAPTER 2 LONG QUESTIONS

Q1. Explain the Chlor-Alkali process with chemical equation and uses of its products.
Process: When electricity is passed through brine (NaCl solution), it decomposes into NaOH, Chlorine, and Hydrogen.

Equation: 2NaCl + 2H2O → 2NaOH + Cl2 + H2

Uses: H2 (Fuel), Cl2 (Water treatment), NaOH (Soap making).
Q2. What is P.O.P? How is it prepared? Why store it in moisture-proof containers?
Definition: Calcium Sulphate Hemihydrate (CaSO4.1/2H2O).

Preparation: By heating Gypsum at 373K.

Moisture Warning: It reacts with water to form hard Gypsum again, making it useless for construction or medical use.
Q3. Describe the importance of pH in digestive system and tooth decay.
Digestion: Stomach produces HCl (pH 1.2). Excess acid causes pain, cured by Antacids.

Tooth Decay: Starts when mouth pH falls below 5.5. Bacteria produce acid from sugar, which corrodes enamel.
Q4. How are Baking Soda and Washing Soda prepared? Give one use each.
Baking Soda (NaHCO3): Prepared by Solvay process using NaCl, NH3, and CO2. Use: For making crispy food.

Washing Soda (Na2CO3.10H2O): Obtained by heating baking soda and recrystallization. Use: Removing permanent hardness of water.
Q5. What is a neutralization reaction? Give two examples from daily life.
Definition: A reaction in which an acid and a base react to form salt and water is called a neutralization reaction.

Equation: NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H2O

Daily Life Examples:
1. Antacids: Taking Milk of Magnesia to neutralize excess stomach acid.
2. Ant Sting: Rubbing baking soda on an ant bite to neutralize the methanoic acid.
Q6. Explain the concept of Water of Crystallisation with an example.
Concept: It is the fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt. It gives shape and sometimes color to crystals.

Example: Copper Sulphate crystals (CuSO4.5H2O) are blue due to 5 molecules of water. When heated, they lose water and turn white.

Other Examples: Washing soda (10H2O) and Gypsum (2H2O).
Q7. Why do HCl, HNO3, etc., show acidic characters in aqueous solutions while alcohol and glucose do not?
Reason: Acidic character depends on the production of Hydrogen ions (H+) in water. HCl and HNO3 dissociate completely in water to release H+ ions.

Alcohol/Glucose: Although they contain hydrogen, they do not ionize or release H+ ions in water. Since there are no free ions, they do not show acidic properties or conduct electricity.
Q8. How does the concentration of Hydronium ions (H3O+) change when a solution of an acid is diluted?
Dilution: Mixing an acid with water is called dilution. It is an highly exothermic process.

Effect on H3O+: On dilution, the number of hydronium ions (H3O+) per unit volume decreases. This results in an increase in the pH value of the solution (making it less acidic).
