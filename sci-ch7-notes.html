PAGE 1
Importance of Reproduction
Definition: Reproduction is the process by which living organisms produce new individuals of their own kind.
Significance: It is not necessary for survival of an individual, but essential for the survival of the species. It ensures continuity of life on Earth.
DNA Copying: The basic event in reproduction is the creation of a DNA copy. Cells use chemical reactions to build copies of their DNA.
PAGE 2
The Role of Variation
DNA copying is not 100% accurate. These small inaccuracies lead to Variations.
Why Variation is Useful? If a population is suited to a particular niche and that niche changes (e.g., temperature rise), variations help some individuals survive. Thus, variation is the basis of evolution.
PAGE 3
Asexual Reproduction - I
1. Fission: Binary fission (Amoeba) and Multiple fission (Plasmodium).
2. Fragmentation: Simply breaking into pieces. Ex: Spirogyra.
3. Regeneration: Ability to grow full organism from cut body parts. Ex: Planaria and Hydra.
PAGE 4
Asexual Reproduction - II
4. Budding: A small outgrowth (bud) develops. Ex: Hydra and Yeast.
5. Spore Formation: Tiny spores in sporangia grow into new plants. Ex: Rhizopus (Bread mould).
6. Vegetative Propagation: New plants from roots, stems, or leaves.
• Stem: Potato, Ginger.
• Leaf: Bryophyllum.
PAGE 5
Sexual Reproduction Logic
It involves two parents. It creates Maximum Variation because it combines DNA from two different individuals.
Germ Cells: To keep chromosome number constant, specialized cells called gametes (half DNA) are formed via meiosis.
PAGE 6
Reproduction in Flowers
Parts: Sepals, Petals, Stamen (Male - Anther & Filament), Carpel (Female - Stigma, Style, Ovary).
Pollination: Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma.
• Self-pollination
• Cross-pollination.
Fertilization: Pollen tube grows to ovary → Male germ cell fuses with Female egg → Zygote is formed.

Vas Deferens: Carries sperms.

Female Reproductive System

What if Egg is not Fertilized?
If fertilization doesn't occur, the thick lining of the uterus breaks down and comes out as blood and mucus. This cycle is called Menstruation (roughly every 28 days).
Placenta: If fertilized, this special tissue provides nutrition (glucose/oxygen) from mother to embryo.

LONG Q1
Why is DNA copying an essential part of the process of reproduction?

Answer: DNA contains information for the inheritance of features from parents to next generation.
1. It ensures that the offspring has the same body design as the parents.
2. It creates the biochemical machinery of the cell.
3. Small inaccuracies during copying lead to variations, which are the basis for evolution.

LONG Q2
How does binary fission differ from multiple fission? Give examples.

Answer:
1. Binary Fission: The parent cell divides into two equal daughter cells. Occurs during favorable conditions. Ex: Amoeba, Leishmania.
2. Multiple Fission: The parent cell divides into many daughter cells simultaneously inside a cyst. Occurs during unfavorable conditions. Ex: Plasmodium.

LONG Q3
What are the advantages of vegetative propagation?

Answer:
1. Plants can bear flowers and fruits earlier than those produced from seeds.
2. It allows the propagation of plants that have lost the capacity to produce seeds (e.g., banana, jasmine, rose).
3. All plants produced are genetically similar to the parent plant, preserving desirable traits.

LONG Q4
Why is variation beneficial to the species but not necessarily for the individual?

Answer: For an individual, a drastic variation might not suit its current environment, leading to its death.
However, for a species, if the environment changes suddenly (e.g., global warming), variations ensure that at least some individuals survive and continue the species' existence. Thus, variation is the key to long-term survival of a species.

LONG Q5
Differentiate between Self-pollination and Cross-pollination.

Answer:
1. Self-pollination: Transfer of pollen from anther to stigma of the same flower or another flower of the same plant. No external agent is usually required.
2. Cross-pollination: Transfer of pollen from anther of one flower to stigma of a flower on another plant of the same species. External agents like wind, water, or insects are required.

LONG Q6
Explain the process of fertilization in a flowering plant with the help of a diagram description.

Answer:
1. After pollen lands on the Stigma, it germinates and grows a Pollen Tube through the style.
2. The pollen tube carries two male gametes to the Ovary and enters the ovule.
3. One male gamete fuses with the female egg cell to form a Zygote (Fertilization).
4. After fertilization, the zygote divides to form an embryo, the ovule becomes a seed, and the ovary ripens into a fruit.

LONG Q7
What are the changes seen in girls and boys at the time of puberty?

Answer: Puberty brings secondary sexual characteristics due to hormones:
Common: Thick hair growth in armpits and genital areas, oily skin, and appearance of pimples.
In Girls: Breast size begins to increase, darkening of nipple skin, and beginning of menstrual cycle.
In Boys: Growth of facial hair (beard/moustaches), deepening/cracking of voice, and penis occasionally begins to become enlarged and erect.

LONG Q8
What is the role of Seminal Vesicles and Prostate Gland in the male reproductive system?

Answer: Along the path of the vas deferens, glands like the prostate and seminal vesicles add their secretions so that:
1. The sperms are now in a fluid medium which makes their transport easier.
2. This fluid provides nutrition (fructose, calcium, and enzymes) to the sperms, helping them stay active and motile for fertilization.

LONG Q9
Explain the process of Menstruation. Why does it occur?

Answer:
1. Every month, the ovary releases one egg and the uterus prepares itself to receive a fertilized egg by making its lining thick and spongy.
2. If the egg is not fertilized, this lining is no longer needed.
3. So, the lining slowly breaks and comes out through the vagina as blood and mucus.
4. This cycle takes place roughly every month and is known as menstruation, lasting for about 2 to 8 days.

LONG Q10
What are the different methods of contraception? Why is family planning important?

Answer: Contraceptive methods include:
1. Barrier Methods: Condoms or diaphragms (prevent sperm meeting egg).
2. Chemical Methods: Oral pills (change hormonal balance to stop egg release).
3. IUCDs: Copper-T (placed in uterus to prevent implantation).
4. Surgical Methods: Vasectomy (males) and Tubectomy (females).

Importance: It prevents unwanted pregnancies, controls population growth, and protects the mother's health by ensuring proper gaps between children.

HARD Q11
Explain the structure and function of the Placenta in human females. Why is it considered a temporary endocrine gland?

Answer:
1. Structure: It is a disc-like tissue embedded in the uterine wall. It contains villi on the embryo's side and blood spaces on the mother's side.
2. Function: It provides a large surface area for glucose and oxygen to pass from mother to embryo, and for waste products (CO2/urea) to be removed from embryo to mother's blood.
3. Endocrine Role: It is called 'temporary' because it is only present during pregnancy and secretes hormones like hCG, Estrogen, and Progesterone to maintain the pregnancy.

HARD Q12
How does the chromosome number remain constant in organisms that reproduce sexually? Explain the process.

Answer: If each generation simply combined DNA from two parents, the DNA amount would double every time. To prevent this:
1. Special cells called Germ-cells (gametes) are formed in reproductive organs through a process called Meiosis.
2. Meiosis reduces the number of chromosomes to half (n). For example, in humans, 46 becomes 23.
3. When a male gamete (23) fuses with a female gamete (23) during fertilization, the resulting Zygote restores the original number (23+23=46).

HARD Q13
Describe the triple fusion and double fertilization process in angiosperms. Why is it called 'Double' fertilization?

Answer: In flowering plants, the pollen tube releases two male gametes into the embryo sac:
1. Syngamy: The 1st male gamete fuses with the Egg cell to form a Zygote (2n).
2. Triple Fusion: The 2nd male gamete fuses with two polar nuclei to form the primary endosperm nucleus (3n).
3. Double Fertilization: Since fertilization occurs twice in the same embryo sac (Syngamy + Triple Fusion), it is called Double Fertilization.

HARD Q14
Contrast the role of 'Testes' and 'Ovaries' as both reproductive organs and endocrine glands.

Answer:
1. Testes (Male):
• Reproductive: Produce Sperms.
• Endocrine: Secretes Testosterone (controls puberty changes and sperm production).
2. Ovaries (Female):
• Reproductive: Produce Eggs (Ova).
• Endocrine: Secretes Estrogen and Progesterone (controls menstrual cycle, puberty changes, and pregnancy maintenance).

HARD Q15
Compare and contrast the process of 'Regeneration' and 'Fragmentation'. Why can't all organisms show regeneration?

Answer:
1. Fragmentation: Occurs in multi-cellular organisms with simple body design (e.g., Spirogyra). The body simply breaks into pieces, and each piece grows into a new individual.
2. Regeneration: Occurs in organisms with specialized cells (e.g., Planaria). If the organism is cut, specialized cells proliferate and make new tissues.
3. Why not all? Complex organisms (like humans) have highly differentiated tissues and organs. Their cells have lost the 'totipotency' (ability to form any cell type), so they can only repair tissues but not regrow whole organs or bodies.

HARD Q16
Trace the path of sperm from the testes to the exterior. Mention the role of each structure involved.

Answer:
1. Testes: Production of sperms.
2. Epididymis: Temporary storage and maturation of sperms.
3. Vas Deferens: Long tube that carries sperms upward towards the abdomen.
4. Glands (Prostate/Seminal Vesicles): Add fluid and nutrients to form Semen.
5. Urethra: Common duct for semen and urine to travel to the outside.
6. Penis: External organ that delivers sperms into the female reproductive tract.

HARD Q17
What is 'Tissue Culture'? Explain the steps involved and its significance in modern agriculture.

Answer: Tissue culture is an artificial vegetative propagation method.
1. Steps: Small tissue/cells are removed from the growing tip → Placed in artificial medium to form a mass of cells (Callus) → Transferred to medium containing hormones for growth and differentiation → Plantlets are placed in soil.
2. Significance: Many plants can be grown from one parent in disease-free conditions. Extremely useful for ornamental plants and orchids.

HARD Q18
Explain how the sexual mode of reproduction leads to greater diversity and evolution compared to asexual reproduction.

Answer:
1. In asexual reproduction, DNA copying is the only source of variation (very limited).
2. In sexual reproduction, two different DNA patterns combine from two different parents.
3. Each new generation is a unique combination of traits, leading to accumulated variations over many generations.
4. This high diversity allows the species to adapt to changing environments, which is the driving force behind Natural Selection and Evolution.
