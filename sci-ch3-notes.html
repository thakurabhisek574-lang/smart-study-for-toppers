CHAPTER 3: METALS & NON-METALS

PAGE 1: PHYSICAL PROPERTIES (METALS)

Physical Nature of Metals

  • Malleability: Gold and Silver can be beaten into ultra-thin foils.
  • Ductility: 1 gram of Gold can be drawn into a 2km long wire.
  • Thermal Conductivity: Silver is the champion, Lead and Mercury are the losers (poor conductors).
  • Density: Generally high, but Li, Na, K have very low density.
PAGE 2: PHYSICAL PROPERTIES (NON-METALS)

The World of Non-Metals

Non-metals are mostly gases (Oxygen) or solids (Sulphur), except Bromine which is a liquid.

  • Lustre: Usually dull, but Iodine shines like a metal.
  • Allotropes: Carbon exists as Diamond (hardest) and Graphite (good conductor).
  • Melting Points: Generally low, but Gallium and Caesium melt on your palm!
PAGE 3: CHEMICAL PROPERTIES I

Reaction with Oxygen

Metals burn in air to form Basic Oxides. Some are special:

4Na + O₂ → 2Na₂O (Room Temp)
2Cu + O₂ → 2CuO (Black Copper Oxide)

Amphoteric Oxides: Those that react with both Acid and Base.

Al₂O₃ + 2NaOH → 2NaAlO₂ (Sodium Aluminate) + H₂O
PAGE 4: CHEMICAL PROPERTIES II

Reaction with Water

  • Sodium/Potassium: React violently, H₂ catches fire immediately.
  • Calcium: Starts floating because H₂ bubbles stick to its surface.
  • Magnesium: Only reacts with hot water.
  • Iron/Aluminium: Only reacts with steam.
3Fe + 4H₂O (steam) → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂
PAGE 5: REACTION WITH ACIDS

Metals + Dilute Acids

Metal + Acid → Salt + Hydrogen gas.

The HNO₃ Exception: When metals react with Nitric Acid, H₂ gas is NOT evolved because HNO₃ is a strong oxidizing agent. It oxidizes H₂ to H₂O.

Exception: Mn and Mg still produce H₂ with very dilute HNO₃.
PAGE 6: REACTIVITY SERIES

The Reactivity Ladder

This list decides who wins a displacement reaction.

K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > [H] > Cu > Hg > Ag > Au
  • Metals above Hydrogen are "Active Metals".
  • Gold (Au) is the most "Noble" (least reactive).
PAGE 7: IONIC BONDING

Formation of Ionic Compounds

Metals lose electrons (become Cations), Non-metals gain electrons (become Anions).

Mg → Mg²⁺ + 2e⁻ (2,8,2 → 2,8)
Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻ (2,8,7 → 2,8,8)
Result: MgCl₂

Properties: High MP/BP, Hard/Brittle, Conductive only when liquid.

PAGE 8: METALLURGY I

Concentration of Ores

  • Minerals: Naturally occurring compounds.
  • Ores: Minerals from which metal can be extracted profitably.
  • Gangue: Dust, sand, and stones mixed with ore.
PAGE 9: METALLURGY II

Roasting and Calcination

Essential for converting ores into oxides.

Roasting (Sulphide Ores): 2ZnS + 3O₂ → 2ZnO + 2SO₂

Calcination (Carbonate Ores): ZnCO₃ → ZnO + CO₂

Oxides are then reduced using Carbon (Coke).

PAGE 10: REDUCTION METHODS

Thermit Reaction & Electrolysis

For highly reactive metals, we use electricity.

Thermit Reaction: Fe₂O₃ + 2Al → 2Fe(l) + Al₂O₃ + Heat

Used to join railway tracks because the Iron produced is in molten state.

PAGE 11: REFINING

Electrolytic Refining of Copper

  • Anode: Impure thick block of Copper.
  • Cathode: Pure thin strip of Copper.
  • Electrolyte: Acidified CuSO₄ solution.
  • Anode Mud: Insoluble impurities settle at the bottom.
PAGE 12: CORROSION & ALLOYS

Protection of Metals

Prevention: Painting, Oiling, Galvanising (Zinc coating), Anodising, and Alloying.

Popular Alloys:

  • Stainless Steel: Fe + Ni + Cr (Never rusts).
  • Brass: Cu + Zn | Bronze: Cu + Sn.
  • 22 Carat Gold: 22 parts Gold + 2 parts Copper/Silver (for strength).

SECTION C: CH-3 LONG QUESTIONS

Q1. What are Amphoteric Oxides? Give two examples with chemical equations.
Definition: Those metal oxides which show both acidic as well as basic behaviour are called amphoteric oxides.

Example 1 (Aluminium Oxide):
Al2O3 + 6HCl → 2AlCl3 + 3H2O (As Base)
Al2O3 + 2NaOH → 2NaAlO2 + H2O (As Acid)

Example 2 (Zinc Oxide):
ZnO + 2HCl → ZnCl2 + H2O
ZnO + 2NaOH → Na2ZnO2 + H2O
Q2. Differentiate between Roasting and Calcination with examples.
Roasting: It is the process of heating sulphide ores strongly in the presence of excess air.
Example: 2ZnS + 3O2 → 2ZnO + 2SO2

Calcination: It is the process of heating carbonate ores strongly in limited air or absence of air.
Example: ZnCO3 → ZnO + CO2

Both processes are used to convert ores into metal oxides for easier reduction.
Q3. Explain the formation of Magnesium Chloride (MgCl2) with electron dot structures.
1. Magnesium (Mg): Atomic number 12. Electronic configuration (2, 8, 2). It loses 2 electrons to form Mg2+.
2. Chlorine (Cl): Atomic number 17. Electronic configuration (2, 8, 7). It needs 1 electron to complete its octet.

Process: One Mg atom transfers its two valence electrons to two Cl atoms (one electron to each).
Result: An ionic bond is formed between Mg2+ and 2Cl- ions, resulting in MgCl2.
Q4. What is the Thermit Reaction? Write its equation and one major application.
Definition: The displacement reaction between Iron(III) oxide and Aluminium is highly exothermic. The heat produced is so high that the iron is obtained in molten state.

Equation: Fe2O3(s) + 2Al(s) → 2Fe(l) + Al2O3(s) + Heat

Application: This reaction is used to join cracked railway tracks and broken machine parts on-site.
Q5. Explain the process of Electrolytic Refining of Copper with a neat diagram description.
Apparatus Setup:
1. Anode: A thick block of impure copper.
2. Cathode: A thin strip of pure copper.
3. Electrolyte: Acidified copper sulphate (CuSO4) solution.

Process: When current is passed, pure copper from the anode dissolves into the electrolyte and an equivalent amount of pure copper from the electrolyte is deposited on the cathode.
Anode Mud: Insoluble impurities settle down below the anode.
Q6. What are Alloys? List the constituents and uses of Brass, Bronze, and Solder.
Definition: An alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal.

1. Brass: Copper (Cu) + Zinc (Zn). Used for making decorative items and hardware.
2. Bronze: Copper (Cu) + Tin (Sn). Used for making statues, coins, and medals.
3. Solder: Lead (Pb) + Tin (Sn). It has a low melting point and is used for welding electrical wires together.
Q7. Why are Ionic Compounds solids and have high melting points? Why do they conduct electricity only in molten state?
High Melting Point: This is because there is a very strong force of attraction (electrostatic force) between the positive and negative ions. A considerable amount of energy is required to break this bond.

Electrical Conductivity: In solid state, ions are not free to move due to rigid structure. However, in molten or aqueous state, the electrostatic forces are weakened by heat/water, allowing ions to move freely and conduct electricity.
Q8. How is Copper extracted from its sulphide ore (Copper Glance)? Write the equations.
Copper is extracted by simply heating the ore in air.

Step 1 (Roasting): 2Cu2S + 3O2 → 2Cu2O + 2SO2
(Copper sulphide is converted to copper oxide).

Step 2 (Self-Reduction): 2Cu2O + Cu2S → 6Cu + SO2
(The remaining copper sulphide reacts with copper oxide to give pure copper metal).
Q9. What is Aqua Regia? Why is it able to dissolve noble metals like Gold and Platinum which even concentrated acids cannot?
Composition: Aqua Regia (Royal Water) is a freshly prepared mixture of Concentrated HCl and Concentrated HNO3 in the ratio of 3:1.

Logic: Neither of these acids can dissolve gold alone. However, the mixture produces nascent chlorine, which is highly reactive. This nascent chlorine reacts with gold to form soluble gold chloride (AuCl3), thus dissolving it. It is a highly corrosive and fuming liquid.
Q10. Explain the chemical process behind the blackening of Silver articles and the green coating on Copper vessels when exposed to air.
1. Silver (Blackening): Silver reacts with Hydrogen Sulphide (H2S) gas present in the air to form a layer of Silver Sulphide (Ag2S). This layer is black in color.

2. Copper (Green Coating): Copper reacts with moist Carbon Dioxide (CO2) in the air and slowly loses its shiny brown surface. It gains a green coat of Basic Copper Carbonate [CuCO3.Cu(OH)2]. This is the corrosion of copper.
Q11. Why is Zinc more reactive than Copper? How can you prove this using a displacement reaction? Give the chemical equation.
In the Reactivity Series, Zinc is placed above Copper, meaning Zinc has a higher tendency to lose electrons and form positive ions.

Experiment: If we put a Zinc strip in a blue-colored Copper Sulphate (CuSO4) solution, the blue color gradually fades and a reddish-brown deposit of copper is seen on the zinc strip.

Equation: Zn(s) + CuSO4(aq) → ZnSO4(aq) + Cu(s)
This proves Zinc displaces Copper because it is more reactive.
Q12. Why can't we reduce Sodium or Magnesium oxides using Carbon? Which method is used instead?
Reason: Metals like Sodium, Magnesium, and Aluminium are highly reactive. These metals have a much higher affinity for oxygen than carbon has. Therefore, carbon cannot "pull" oxygen away from these metal oxides.

Method: They are obtained by Electrolytic Reduction of their molten chlorides. For example, in Sodium Chloride electrolysis:
• At Cathode: Na+ + e- → Na (Metal deposited)
• At Anode: 2Cl- → Cl2 + 2e- (Gas evolved)
Q13. What are Alloys? How does alloying change the properties of pure metals? Explain with the example of Iron.
An Alloy is a homogeneous mixture of two or more metals, or a metal and a non-metal. It is prepared by melting the primary metal and then dissolving other elements in it.

Iron Example: Pure iron is very soft and stretches easily when hot. But:
1. If mixed with 0.05% Carbon, it becomes hard and strong.
2. If mixed with Nickel and Chromium, we get Stainless Steel, which is hard and does not rust.

Alloying improves hardness, electrical conductivity, and resistance to corrosion.
Q14. What is Galvanisation? How does it protect iron even if the zinc coating is broken?
Definition: Galvanisation is a method of protecting steel and iron from rusting by coating them with a thin layer of Zinc.

The Logic: Even if the zinc coating is scratched or broken, the iron underneath does not rust. This is because Zinc is more reactive than Iron. In the presence of moisture, Zinc undergoes oxidation preferentially (sacrificial protection), saving the iron from reacting with oxygen and water.
Q15. Distinguish between Calcination and Roasting. Why are these processes carried out?
Roasting: It is used for Sulphide ores. The ore is heated strongly in the presence of excess air. (Example: 2ZnS + 3O2 → 2ZnO + 2SO2)

Calcination: It is used for Carbonate ores. The ore is heated strongly in limited air or absence of air. (Example: ZnCO3 → ZnO + CO2)

Purpose: Both processes are carried out to convert metal ores into their oxides, which can then be easily reduced to get the pure metal.
Q16. Why does Magnesium start floating when it is reacted with hot water? Explain with the reaction.
Magnesium does not react with cold water; it reacts only with hot water to form magnesium hydroxide and hydrogen gas.

Reaction: Mg(s) + 2H2O(l) → Mg(OH)2(aq) + H2(g)

Floating Reason: The bubbles of Hydrogen gas formed during the reaction stick to the surface of the magnesium metal. These gas bubbles act like balloons, making the metal light enough to float on the surface of the water.
Q17. How are metals of low reactivity (like Mercury) extracted from their ores? Explain with the example of Cinnabar.
Metals of low reactivity are very unreactive. The oxides of these metals can be reduced to metals by heating alone.

Cinnabar (HgS) is the ore of mercury. When it is heated in air, it is first converted into mercuric oxide (HgO):
2HgS + 3O2 → 2HgO + 2SO2

Mercuric oxide is then reduced to mercury on further heating:
2HgO → 2Hg + O2
Q18. State three general properties of Ionic Compounds. Why do they conduct electricity only in molten or solution state?
Properties:
1. They are solid and hard due to strong attraction between ions.
2. They have high melting and boiling points.
3. They are generally soluble in water but insoluble in petrol/kerosene.

Electrical Conductivity: In solid state, ions are held in a fixed position by strong forces and cannot move. In molten or solution state, these forces are overcome by heat or water, allowing ions to move freely and conduct electricity.
