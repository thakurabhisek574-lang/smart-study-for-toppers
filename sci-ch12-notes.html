1. Variations and Reproduction

Introduction: Bachpan se humne dekha hai ki hum apne parents jaise dikhte hain, par bilkul unki copy nahi hote. Is study ko Genetics kehte hain.

Asexual vs Sexual Variation: Asexual reproduction mein variations bahut kam hoti hain (sirf DNA copying errors ki wajah se). Lekin Sexual reproduction mein variations bahut zyada hoti hain kyunki do alag parents ka DNA milta hai.

Importance: Variations species ko survive karne mein madad karti hain. Agar environment suddenly badal jaye, toh variations hi kisi species ko khatam hone se bachati hain.

2. Gregor Mendel's Experiments

Mendel ne Garden Pea (Pisum sativum) par kaam kiya. Unhone 7 saal tak research ki aur bataya ki characters kaise inherit hote hain.

Why Pea Plant?

  • Bisexual flowers aur easy self-pollination.
  • Chota life cycle (jaldi results milte hain).
  • Saaf dikhne wale contrasting characters (Tall/Dwarf).

HeightTall (T)Dwarf (t)
Seed ShapeRound (R)Wrinkled (r)
Seed ColorYellow (Y)Green (y)

3. Mendel's Three Great Laws

I. Law of Dominance

Jab do alag traits (T aur t) saath aate hain, toh sirf dominant trait (Tall) hi dikhta hai. Recessive trait (Dwarf) tab tak nahi dikhta jab tak wo pair mein na ho (tt).

II. Law of Segregation

Jab gametes (seeds) bante hain, toh genes ka pair alag-alag ho jata hai. Har sperm ya egg mein sirf ek hi gene jata hai. Ise 'Purity of Gametes' bhi kehte hain.

III. Law of Independent Assortment

Ye Dihybrid cross par base hai. Iska matlab hai ki seed ka color aur seed ki shape ek dusre par depend nahi karte. Dono azadi se inherit hote hain.

PAGE 13-16

4. Monohybrid & Dihybrid Cross

Monohybrid (Single Trait): F2 generation ka ratio hamesha 3 : 1 (Phenotype) aur 1 : 2 : 1 (Genotype) hota hai.

Dihybrid (Two Traits): Jab Round-Yellow aur Wrinkled-Green ko cross kiya gaya, toh F2 mein 9 : 3 : 3 : 1 ka ratio mila.

PAGE 17-20

5. Sex Determination

Humans mein 23 pairs chromosomes hote hain. 22 pairs sabme same hote hain (Autosomes), lekin 23rd pair sex decide karta hai.

  • Female: XX chromosomes hote hain.
  • Male: XY chromosomes hote hain.

Logic: Baby boy hoga ya girl, ye puri tarah **Father** par depend karta hai. Agar father se 'Y' mila toh Boy, agar 'X' mila toh Girl.

PAGE 21+

6. Expression of Traits

DNA cells ke nucleus mein hota hai. DNA ka wo part jo ek protein banane ki information deta hai, use **Gene** kehte hain. Protein hi decide karta hai ki hamara body design ya character kaisa hoga.

Acquired Traits: Jo hum seekhte hain (Swimming), ye DNA mein nahi jate.

Inherited Traits: Jo humein parents se milte hain (Eye color), ye DNA ke zariye pass hote hain.

📝 HEREDITY: TOP 10 LONG QUESTIONS

#MONOHYBRID_CROSS 1. Explain Mendel's Monohybrid Cross with an example and Punnett Square.
Mendel crossed a pure tall (TT) and a pure dwarf (tt) pea plant.
• F1 Generation: All plants were heterozygous Tall (Tt).
• F2 Generation: After selfing F1, the result was 3 Tall : 1 Dwarf (Phenotypic ratio) and 1(TT):2(Tt):1(tt) (Genotypic ratio).
Conclusion: This experiment led to the 'Law of Segregation', stating that alleles separate during gamete formation.
#DIHYBRID_CROSS 2. State the Law of Independent Assortment with the help of a Dihybrid cross.
This law states that when two pairs of traits are combined in a hybrid, the segregation of one pair of characters is independent of the other pair.
Mendel used Round-Yellow (RRYY) and Wrinkled-Green (rryy) seeds.
The F2 phenotypic ratio was 9:3:3:1. This showed that new combinations like Round-Green and Wrinkled-Yellow appear, proving independence.
#SEX_DETERMINATION 3. Explain the mechanism of sex determination in human beings.
Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes. The 23rd pair is the sex chromosome.
• Females have XX and Males have XY.
• All eggs carry an 'X' chromosome. Sperms can carry either an 'X' or a 'Y'.
• If X-sperm + X-egg = Female (XX).
• If Y-sperm + X-egg = Male (XY).
Important: The father is responsible for the sex of the child.
#ACQUIRED_VS_INHERITED 4. Differentiate between Acquired and Inherited traits with examples.
Inherited Traits: These are passed from parents to offspring via DNA (e.g., Eye color, Skin tone). They cause evolution.
Acquired Traits: These are developed during a lifetime due to environment or use/disuse (e.g., Learning to play guitar, weight gain).
Acquired traits do not change the DNA of germ cells, so they are not passed to the next generation.
#SPECIATION 5. What is Speciation? What factors lead to the formation of a new species?
Speciation is the formation of a new species from an existing one.
Factors:
1. Geographical Isolation: Populations separated by mountains/rivers.
2. Genetic Drift: Random changes in gene frequency.
3. Natural Selection: Nature picking the best-suited variations.
4. Reproductive Isolation: When two groups can no longer interbreed.
#ORGANS_EVIDENCE 6. Compare Homologous and Analogous organs as evidence for evolution.
Homologous Organs: Similar basic structure but different functions (e.g., Forelimbs of lizard, frog, and human). They prove Divergent Evolution (common ancestors).
Analogous Organs: Different structure but similar function (e.g., Wings of bird and bat). They prove Convergent Evolution (adapting to same environment).
#FOSSILS 7. What are fossils? How do they help in tracing evolutionary history?
Fossils are the preserved remains or impressions of organisms that lived in the past.
Importance:
1. They show the timeline of evolution.
2. They show 'connecting links' (e.g., Archaeopteryx links reptiles to birds).
3. They help us understand which organisms were simpler and which were complex.
#PROTEIN_EXPRESSION 8. How do genes control the expression of traits?
A gene is a segment of DNA that provides instructions for making a specific protein.
• For example, a gene for plant height produces an enzyme (protein) that makes growth hormone.
• If the gene works efficiently, more hormone is produced → Tall plant.
• If the gene is altered, less hormone is produced → Dwarf plant.
Thus, DNA controls traits via protein synthesis.
#EVOLUTION_STAGES 9. Explain evolution by stages using the example of the eye or feathers.
Complex organs like the eye are not created by a single DNA change but by bit-by-bit changes over generations.
• Eye: Started as simple eye-spots in Planaria (detected light), then became complex.
• Feathers: Initially evolved for insulation (warmth) in dinosaurs, but later were adapted for flight in birds.
#ARTIFICIAL_SELECTION 10. How does artificial selection differ from natural selection? Explain with wild cabbage.
Natural Selection: Nature selects traits for survival.
Artificial Selection: Humans select traits for their own benefit.
Example: From Wild Cabbage, humans selected:
• Short leaves → Cabbage
• Arrested flowers → Broccoli
• Sterile flowers → Cauliflower
• Swollen parts → Kohlrabi

#GENETIC_PROBABILITY 11. A man with blood group A marries a woman with blood group B. They have a child with blood group O. Show the possibility of all other blood groups in their future children using a cross.
Child has 'O' group (ii), which means both parents must carry the 'i' allele.
• Genotypes: Father is IAi and Mother is IBi.
• Cross Results:
1. IAIB (Group AB) - 25%
2. IAi (Group A) - 25%
3. IBi (Group B) - 25%
4. ii (Group O) - 25%
Conclusion: In this specific case, all four blood groups (A, B, AB, O) are possible in their children.
#DNA_VS_VARIATION 12. "DNA copying is not a foolproof process." Justify this statement and explain its significance in the survival of a species.
During cell division, DNA replication occurs. Biochemical reactions involved are not 100% accurate, leading to small variations in the new DNA strand.
Significance:
1. If environment changes suddenly (e.g., global warming), some variants might survive while others perish.
2. It provides the raw material for Evolution.
3. Without these "errors," species would be static and highly vulnerable to extinction.
#DIHYBRID_ANALYSIS 13. In a Dihybrid cross, what is the frequency of 'Double Homozygous Recessive' (rryy) and 'Double Homozygous Dominant' (RRYY) offsprings in F2? Explain.
In a standard 16-box Punnett Square:
• RRYY (Pure Dominant): Only 1/16 (Top-left corner).
• rryy (Pure Recessive): Only 1/16 (Bottom-right corner).
Concept: Both parental pure types appear with the same frequency (6.25% each), while the rest 14/16 are either heterozygous or recombinants.
#GEOGRAPHICAL_ISOLATION 14. Will geographical isolation be a major factor in the speciation of a self-pollinating plant species? Why or why not?
Answer: No.
Speciation via geographical isolation mainly affects outbreeding/sexually reproducing organisms where gene flow is cut off.
In self-pollinating plants, the pollen comes from the same plant, so there is already very little gene flow between different plants. Variations occur mainly due to internal mutations rather than external genetic exchange.
#MOLECULAR_PHYLOGENY 15. Explain how Molecular Phylogeny helps in tracing evolutionary relationships better than Morphology.
Morphology (looking at structures) can be misleading because of Analogous organs (different ancestors, same look).
Molecular Phylogeny:
1. It compares changes in DNA or Protein sequences.
2. More distantly related organisms have more differences in their DNA.
3. This gives a direct quantitative measure of how long ago two species diverged, which morphology cannot do accurately.
#CONNECTING_LINKS 16. Mention the importance of Archaeopteryx in evolution. Which two classes does it bridge?
Archaeopteryx is a Connecting Link fossil.
• Reptilian features: It had teeth in its beak, a long bony tail, and claws on its wings.
• Avian (Bird) features: It had feathers and wings.
Conclusion: It provides solid evidence that birds have evolved from reptiles.
#GENETIC_DRIFT_HARD 17. Explain the "Beetle Population" example from NCERT to describe Genetic Drift and Natural Selection.
1. Natural Selection (Green vs Red): Crows eat red beetles. A mutation creates green beetles. Crows can't see green on green leaves. Green beetles survive better. (Directional change).
2. Genetic Drift (Blue vs Red): An elephant steps on the beetle bush, accidentally killing most red ones. The few surviving blue ones (lucky, not better) multiply. (Random change in small population).
#REPRODUCTIVE_BARRIERS 18. Why can a lion and a tiger not be considered the same species, even though they look similar and belong to the same genus (Panthera)?
According to the Biological Species Concept:
1. Species must be able to interbreed in nature.
2. They must produce fertile offspring.
Lions and Tigers are reproductively isolated in the wild. Although they can produce "Ligers" or "Tigons" in captivity, these hybrids are usually sterile. Hence, they are distinct species.
