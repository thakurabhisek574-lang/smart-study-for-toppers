ELECTRICITY ⚡

PAGE 1

Concept of Electric Charge (Q)

Charge atom ki property hai. Ye do type ke hote hain: Positive (+) aur Negative (-). Same charges repel karte hain, opposite attract.
Q = n × e

(n = No. of electrons, e = 1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)

PAGE 2

Electric Current (I)

"Rate of flow of charge" ko current kehte hain. Current hamesha high potential se low potential ki taraf jata hai.
I = Q / t

Unit: Ampere (A). Isko measure karne ke liye Ammeter use hota hai.

PAGE 3

Potential Difference (V)

Ek unit charge ko ek point se dusre point tak move karne mein jo kaam (Work) hota hai.
V = W / Q

Unit: Volt (V). Isko measure karne ke liye Voltmeter parallel mein lagate hain.

PAGE 4

Ohm's Law (The Heart of Chapter)

Temperature constant rahe toh current (I) hamesha voltage (V) ke directly proportional hota hai.
V = I × R
Yahan R Resistance hai jo har material ke liye fixed (constant) hota hai. Iska graph hamesha ek Straight Line hota hai.
PAGE 5

Resistance (R) & Factors

Current ke raaste mein aane wali rukawat ko Resistance kehte hain.
  • Length (L) badhne se R badhta hai.
  • Area (A) badhne se R kam hota hai.
  • Material aur Temperature par depend karta hai.
PAGE 6

Resistivity (ρ - Rho)

Ye material ki intrinsic property hai. Ye shape ya size par depend nahi karti.
R = ρ (L / A)

Unit: Ωm (Ohm-meter). Alloys ki resistivity metals se zyada hoti hai.

PAGE 7

Series Combination

Jab resistors back-to-back ek hi line mein jude hon. Isme Current (I) same rehta hai.
Rₛ = R₁ + R₂ + R₃

Isme total resistance sabse bade resistance se bhi zyada aata hai.

PAGE 8

Parallel Combination

Gharon mein yahi use hota hai. Isme Voltage (V) same rehta hai aur current divide ho jata hai.
1/Rₚ = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃

Isme total resistance sabse chote resistance se bhi kam ho jata hai.

PAGE 9

Heating Effect of Current

Jab current kisi high resistance wire se guzarta hai, toh wire garam ho jata hai.
H = I²Rt

Isko Joule's Law of Heating bhi kehte hain.

PAGE 10

Applications of Heating

Bulb, Heater, Iron, aur Fuse isi concept par chalte hain.

Bulb: Tungsten (High melting point: 3380°C) use hota hai.
Fuse: Safety device jo over-current par pighal jata hai.

PAGE 11-15

Electric Power & Energy (Pro Tips)

Board exams mein yahan se numerical pakka aayega!
P = V × I = I²R = V²/R

Commercial Unit of Energy: 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ Joules (Isko 1 unit kehte hain).

Numerical karte waqt power ko kW mein aur time ko Hour mein convert karna mat bhulna, tabhi Bill niklega!

⚡ TOP 10 LONG QUESTIONS (DETAILED) ⚡

QUESTION 01

State Ohm’s Law. How can it be verified experimentally? Draw the circuit diagram.

1. Statement:

Ohm's law states that the current (I) flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (V) across its ends, provided temperature and other physical conditions remain constant. ($V = IR$)

2. Experimental Verification:

  • Connect a battery, an ammeter, a voltmeter, a rheostat, and a resistor in a circuit.
  • Change the current using the rheostat and note the corresponding readings of V and I.
  • The ratio $V/I$ will always be constant, giving a straight-line graph.

3. Circuit Diagram:

[Battery] -- [Ammeter] -- [Resistor (Voltmeter in Parallel)] -- [Rheostat] -- [Switch]

QUESTION 02

Derive the formula for equivalent resistance when three resistors are connected in Series.

1. Assumption:

Let three resistors $R_1, R_2, R_3$ be connected in series with a battery of voltage $V$.

2. Key Points:

Current (I) is the same through all resistors. Total Voltage $V = V_1 + V_2 + V_3$.

3. Derivation:

From Ohm's Law: $V_1 = IR_1, V_2 = IR_2, V_3 = IR_3$.
Substitute in total voltage: $V = IR_1 + IR_2 + IR_3$
$IR_s = I(R_1 + R_2 + R_3)$
$R_s = R_1 + R_2 + R_3$

QUESTION 03

Derive the formula for equivalent resistance when three resistors are connected in Parallel.

1. Key Points:

Voltage (V) is the same across all resistors. Total Current $I = I_1 + I_2 + I_3$.

2. Derivation:

From Ohm's Law: $I_1 = V/R_1, I_2 = V/R_2, I_3 = V/R_3$.
Total Current $I = V/R_1 + V/R_2 + V/R_3$
$V/R_p = V(1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + 1/R_3)$
$1/R_p = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + 1/R_3$

QUESTION 04

What is Joule’s Heating Effect? State the laws and list three practical applications.

1. Definition:

When current passes through a high-resistance wire, electrical energy is converted into heat energy. This is Joule's heating effect.

2. Mathematical Laws:

$H = I^2Rt$. Heat is directly proportional to:
(a) Square of current ($I^2$)
(b) Resistance ($R$)
(c) Time ($t$)

3. Applications:

  • Electric Iron/Heater (Heating element)
  • Electric Bulb (Tungsten filament)
  • Electric Fuse (Safety device)
QUESTION 05

Explain the factors on which the resistance of a conductor depends. Define Resistivity.

1. Factors:

  • Length (L): $R \propto L$
  • Area of Cross-section (A): $R \propto 1/A$
  • Nature of Material: Different for different substances.
  • Temperature: Increases with temperature for metals.

2. Resistivity ($\rho$):

It is the resistance of a conductor of unit length and unit area of cross-section. SI Unit: $\Omega \cdot m$. It only depends on material and temperature, not on dimensions.

QUESTION 06

Why is a parallel arrangement used in domestic wiring instead of series? (List 4 reasons)

  1. Independent Switches: Each appliance can be turned ON/OFF separately.
  2. Same Voltage: Every appliance gets the full 220V supply.
  3. Reliability: If one appliance fails, others keep working.
  4. Low Net Resistance: Overall resistance is low, allowing enough current flow.
QUESTION 07

Define Electric Power. Derive its three different formulas ($VI, I^2R, V^2/R$).

Power (P) is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed.

  • $P = W/t = VQ/t = VI$ (Since $I = Q/t$)
  • Substitute $V = IR$: $P = (IR) \times I = I^2R$
  • Substitute $I = V/R$: $P = V \times (V/R) = V^2/R$
QUESTION 08

How does an Electric Fuse work? Why is its rating important?

Working:

A fuse is a safety device made of a metal/alloy with a low melting point. When current exceeds a safe limit, the fuse wire melts due to Joule's heating, breaking the circuit.

Rating:

Fuse rating (e.g., 5A) must be slightly higher than the appliance's normal current but lower than the wire's limit to prevent fire.

QUESTION 09

A 100W bulb and a 60W bulb are connected in parallel to 220V. Find the total current drawn.

Step 1: Current in Bulb 1 ($I_1$) = $P_1/V = 100/220 = 0.45A$

Step 2: Current in Bulb 2 ($I_2$) = $P_2/V = 60/220 = 0.27A$

Step 3: Total Current ($I$) = $I_1 + I_2 = 0.45 + 0.27 = 0.72A$

QUESTION 10

Why does the cord of an electric heater not glow while the heating element does?

The cord is made of copper (low resistance), so very little heat is produced. The heating element is made of Nichrome (high resistance), which produces intense heat ($H \propto R$), causing it to become red hot and glow.

HARDCORE QUESTION 11

A wire of resistance $R$ is cut into five equal parts. These parts are then connected in parallel. If the equivalent resistance of this combination is $R'$, find the ratio $R/R'$.

Step 1: Resistance of each part = $R/5$.

Step 2: In parallel: $1/R' = 1/(R/5) + 1/(R/5) + 1/(R/5) + 1/(R/5) + 1/(R/5)$

Step 3: $1/R' = 5/R + 5/R + 5/R + 5/R + 5/R = 25/R$

Step 4: $R' = R/25 \implies R/R' = 25$.

Final Answer: 25

HARDCORE QUESTION 12

Two lamps, one rated 100W at 220V, and the other 60W at 220V, are connected in parallel to electric mains supply. What current is drawn from the line if the supply voltage is 220V?

Current in 100W Lamp: $I_1 = P_1/V = 100/220 = 5/11 A$

Current in 60W Lamp: $I_2 = P_2/V = 60/220 = 3/11 A$

Total Current: $I = I_1 + I_2 = 5/11 + 3/11 = 8/11 A \approx 0.727 A$.

HARDCORE QUESTION 13

Compare the power used in the 2$\Omega$ resistor in each of the following circuits: (i) a 6V battery in series with 1$\Omega$ and 2$\Omega$ resistors, and (ii) a 4V battery in parallel with 12$\Omega$ and 2$\Omega$ resistors.

Case (i): Series. $R_{total} = 1 + 2 = 3\Omega$. $I = 6/3 = 2A$. Power in 2$\Omega = I^2R = 2^2 \times 2 = 8W$.

Case (ii): Parallel. Voltage across 2$\Omega$ is 4V. Power in 2$\Omega = V^2/R = 4^2 / 2 = 16/2 = 8W$.

Both cases use 8W of power. Ratio is 1:1.

HARDCORE QUESTION 14

A current of 1A flows in a series circuit containing an electric lamp and a conductor of 5$\Omega$ when connected to a 10V battery. Calculate the resistance of the electric lamp. Now if a resistance of 10$\Omega$ is connected in parallel with this series combination, what change (if any) in current flowing through 5$\Omega$ conductor and potential difference across the lamp will take place? Give reason.

Part 1: $V=10V, I=1A$. Total $R = V/I = 10/1 = 10\Omega$. Lamp resistance $R_L = 10 - 5 = 5\Omega$.

Part 2: If 10$\Omega$ is connected in parallel to the whole 10V battery branch, the original branch (Lamp + 5$\Omega$ resistor) still sees 10V. Therefore, NO CHANGE in current (1A) or P.D. (5V) across the lamp because parallel branches are independent.

HARDCORE QUESTION 15

Three resistors of 2$\Omega$, 3$\Omega$, and 6$\Omega$ are given. How will you connect them to get a total resistance of (a) 4$\Omega$ and (b) 1$\Omega$?

(a) For 4$\Omega$: Connect 3$\Omega$ and 6$\Omega$ in parallel, then this set in series with 2$\Omega$.
Parallel of 3 & 6 = $(3 \times 6)/(3 + 6) = 2\Omega$. Total = $2 + 2 = 4\Omega$.

(b) For 1$\Omega$: Connect all three in parallel.
$1/R_p = 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/6 = (3 + 2 + 1)/6 = 6/6 = 1\Omega$.

HARDCORE QUESTION 16

Two wires of equal length, one of copper and the other of manganin (an alloy), have the same thickness. Which one can be used for (i) electrical transmission lines and (ii) heating elements? Why?

(i) Transmission: Copper. It has very low resistivity, so energy loss as heat is minimal.

(ii) Heating Element: Manganin. It has high resistivity and does not oxidize (burn) easily even at high temperatures.

HARDCORE QUESTION 17

An electric heater of resistance 8$\Omega$ draws 15A from the service mains for 2 hours. Calculate the rate at which heat is developed in the heater.

Warning: "Rate of heat developed" means Power (P), not total heat energy (H).

Calculation: $P = I^2R$

$P = (15)^2 \times 8 = 225 \times 8 = 1800 W$ (or J/s).

Final Answer: 1800 J/s

HARDCORE QUESTION 18

Why does the resistance of a conductor increase with an increase in temperature? (Molecular View)

When temperature increases, the positive ions (kernels) in the conductor vibrate more vigorously. This leads to more frequent collisions between the flowing electrons and the vibrating ions. These collisions obstruct the flow of electrons, hence Resistance increases.
